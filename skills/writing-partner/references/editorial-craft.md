# Editorial craft toolkit

This is the diagnostic layer behind the editor persona in SKILL.md. The persona tells you who to be. This tells you what to actually look for. Read the shared method first, then pull the half that matches the genre. Do not dump the whole file at the writer. Use it to find the real problem, then say the one thing that matters.

## The shared method: read it as a reader

Before you diagnose anything, read the piece the way a first-time reader would, and watch your own attention. The single most useful thing you can report is the exact place where your attention died, because the writer cannot feel that on their own page.

- Mark where you drifted, skimmed, or had to reread. That spot is the problem, not wherever the writer thinks it is.
- Ask of every beat, paragraph, scene, and line: does this serve the throughline? If it does not, it is a candidate for cutting, however good it is on its own.
- If you were bored, say so. Boredom is the most honest signal you have. If a section bored you, it will bore the reader, and no amount of polish fixes a section that should not exist.
- Separate two questions that writers blur together: is this good writing, and does this earn its place. A passage can be beautiful and still be dead weight.

Then say the thing as a reader, not as a rubric. "I lost the thread on page three" beats "the structure could be tightened."

## Nonfiction

### The throughline of the idea

Every piece is about one thing. Find it, say it back in a sentence, and test every part against it. If you cannot say what the piece is about in a sentence, the writer probably cannot either, and that is the first problem to solve. If the throughline is weak or generic, do not just defend a muddy spine. Propose a sharper one. The strongest throughline is usually the most specific and the most theirs, the claim only this writer, with this experience, could make.

### Earn the claim

A claim needs evidence, logic, or a concrete example standing under it. When the writing asserts more than it has shown, name it: "you are asking me to believe this and you have not given me a reason yet." Watch for the move where a strong sentence smuggles in an unproven leap. The fix is rarely softening the claim. It is usually adding the thing that earns it.

### Momentum, and where nonfiction sags

Nonfiction dies in specific ways: listing without building, lecturing, over-explaining a point the reader already got, or stretching a stretch with no stakes so the reader has no reason to care. Each section should make the reader want the next one. When a section sags, the question to ask is "why would a reader turn to the next paragraph here," and if you cannot answer it, that is the sag.

### Specifics and people over abstraction

A person at a kitchen table beats a sentence about a category of people. A real number beats "significant." When the writer reaches for an abstraction, push for the specific underneath it. Concrete detail is also what makes a fact stick: a reader remembers why something matters, not the bare claim. This matches how many writers work at their best, opening with a person or a scene and escalating to the structural point.

### Openings, and bottom-line-first versus the slow reveal

Know the difference. A status update or a memo leads with the point, because the reader wants the answer and the detail can wait. A narrative essay or a book chapter is allowed to withhold, because the journey to the payoff is half the value and giving it away in line one kills the reason to read. Do not force the bottom-line-first habit onto narrative nonfiction, and do not let a piece that needed a sharp lead wander for three paragraphs first. Match the opening to the form.

### Section hooks

Momentum across a long piece comes partly from how sections end. A small hook at the end of a section, a question, a turn, a line that points at what is coming, gives the reader a reason to keep going. It should not announce itself. It just has to connect.

## Fiction

### The throughline of the story

Plot, character, and theme pull on the same thread. The plot is what happens, the character is who it happens to and how they change, and the theme is why it mattered, the thing the reader carries out. Keep all three in view. A scene that advances the plot but flattens the character, or lands an event with no bearing on the theme, is usually a scene that needs rework, not just trimming.

### Narrative drive: manage the information

Page-turning is largely about what the reader knows versus what the character knows, and when you reveal it. There are three settings. Mystery: the character knows more than the reader, who reads on to find out. Dramatic irony: the reader knows more than the character, and reads on dreading or anticipating the moment it lands. Suspense: reader and character know the same thing and face it together. Each raises a different question in the reader and pulls them forward. The craft is choosing which one a scene runs on and revealing information at the moment that keeps the question alive, not a page too early.

### Tension on every page

Think of tension as a thread pulled taut from one page to the next. It does not require action or conflict in the loud sense; it requires something unresolved that the reader wants resolved. The thread snaps when a conflict turns out to be silly, unbelievable, or solved too easily, and once it snaps the reader is free to leave. Believable beats big. A small real problem holds tension better than a huge fake one.

### Pacing and the sagging middle

Pacing follows an arc, not a flatline and not random spikes. The opening moves quickly enough to hook and is not the place for long backstory or worldbuilding. The middle is where books sag, usually because the stakes stop rising or the goal goes fuzzy. The climax should not feel rushed. The clearest signal in revision is the place readers stop, and if several readers stop at the same spot, that section drags, full stop.

### Scene and chapter endings

A scene or chapter break is a natural place for the reader to put the book down, which makes it the best place to make stopping hard. End on a high-impact beat, an unanswered question, a reversal, a line that demands the next page. Even if a scene drifts to a soft close in the draft, the ending can be moved to the sharpest moment in revision.

### Earned emotion: show, do not tell

An emotion the writing asserts does not land. An emotion the scene builds does. When a moment tells you a character is devastated, ask whether the scene earned it, and if not, the fix is the scene, not a stronger adjective. The same goes for stakes: a spike in danger or feeling that was not set up reads as melodrama.

### Character: consistency, motivation, arc

Characters need to want something, act in ways that follow from who they are, and change across the story. Watch for a character whose personality shifts with no cause, a motivation that evaporates when the plot needs it to, or an arc that does not actually move. Inconsistent characters are one of the things a reader feels as "off" even when they cannot name it.

### Voice and POV

Hold the point of view steady unless a shift is deliberate and signalled. And remember the hard rule from SKILL.md: characters have their own voices, distinct from each other and from the writer's. A character's dialogue is not the place for the writer's personal voice profile. Each voice should be consistent with that character, not with the author.
