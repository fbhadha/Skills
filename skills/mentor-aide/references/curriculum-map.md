# Curriculum map

This is the what-to-teach-and-track reference. It maps the foundational knowledge and the workbook, lays out the phases for a jump-maker, and explains how to build a tailored program for an established TPM. The full text of both documents lives in the mentor's vault. Read them when you need depth.

## The handbook foundations

The handbook "From technical to business" is your knowledge base, the ideas you reason from. You do not teach it in order. You pull the right idea when the moment calls for it. The fourteen ideas, in short:

1. Correct stops being the job. The move from being right to being trusted.
2. Measure the outcome, not the output. Did a number that matters move, or did you just ship.
3. Set expectations early. Name what good looks like before the work, not after.
4. Write it down. A call that lives only in people's heads is not settled.
5. Trust is your new formula. Credibility, reliability, and closeness over self-interest.
6. Saying no. Protect trust by saying it well and early, with a real alternative.
7. Do the next job before you're paid for it. The manager's loop and the delegation ladder.
8. You grow by doing, not by reading. The 70-20-10 split and the three Es.
9. Say the point first. Lead with the headline, drop the justification once they stop re-checking you.
10. Run the meeting. A room that reaches a decision and ends with owned actions.
11. Make decent calls when you can't be sure. Reversible or not, the premortem, disagree and commit.
12. Figure out who actually decides. RACI and the real owner.
13. Find the critical path. The handoffs and the one nobody is tracking.
14. Carry a story, not a résumé. The thread that ties the stops together.

## The workbook map

The workbook is the toolbox the mentee fills and keeps. Use it to know where they are and what comes next.

Part 1, before you have the title. The climb, building toward the role, and the making-the-move conversion. Open-ended.
Part 2, your first 90 days. The landing once they hold the title. Three blocks, month by month.
Part 3, after the transition. Running the role, and landing a new program fast.
Part 4, tools you keep updating. Stakeholder map, ladder tracker, decision log, success measures, dependency and critical-path map.
Part 5, playbooks for the hard moments. Before an update, before a meeting, saying no, when a project slips, before committing to a date, when everything is urgent.
Part 6, the career thread.

## The phases for a jump-maker

The climb. Before the role, open-ended. The work is doing TPM-shaped tasks in their current seat to build skills and evidence, and building a sponsor. Track readiness, not days. The standing tools in Part 4 are already useful here, because they are already doing the work. The only part gated to later is the first-ninety-days landing.

The move. The conversion to the title, a single push. A promotion, a transfer, or an outside job. The making-the-move section of the workbook is the playbook.

The first 90 days. Starts the day they hold the title. Now it is time-paced against the three blocks.

Running the role. Steady state.

Be honest that the climb does not guarantee the role. Doing the work builds the case, but the title still needs an opening and a sponsor. Part of your job is the honest read on whether that is reachable where they are, or whether the move means leaving.

## Building a program for an established TPM

An established TPM has made the jump, so the foundation is largely review. Do not run the standard arc. Instead:

Run a diagnostic at setup. Collect where they are actually stuck, what their role is demanding that they are not yet good at, and what their goal is.

Compose a tailored program file from the foundations. Skim what they already have. Go deeper on the foundational ideas where they are weak. Add new focus areas the existing material does not cover, like leading other TPMs, org-level strategy, or program scale.

Keep it living. As new problems surface in sessions, fold them in. The program is organized by their problems and runs as long as it needs, so do not force the twelve-session shape onto it.

Build any new material from the foundations and sound practice, and flag it for the mentor before it lands in a file. Never invent it from nowhere. When a new addition proves out and the mentor has vetted it, it can graduate into the workbook's "after the transition" section, so the library grows from real engagements and the next tailored program has more to draw on.
