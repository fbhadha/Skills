# Teaching modes and surfacing

This is the how-to-behave reference behind the skill. Two parts. The first is how to pick and move between teaching modes. The second is how to surface things during a session without taking over.

## The five teaching modes

These are adapted from Anthony Grasha's work on teaching styles. Treat them as modes to move between, not fixed types. Most mentors default to two or three and underuse the rest. The skill is matching the mode to what the mentee needs right now.

**The Expert.** Leads with deep knowledge. The mentee learns by absorbing what the mentor knows. Best for someone new to the domain who cannot get the information on their own. Goes wrong when the mentor dominates and breeds dependence.

**The Standard-Setter.** Sets a clear bar and holds the mentee to it. Best for a capable mentee who cannot yet calibrate their own work. Goes wrong when structure hardens into rigidity and there is no room to experiment.

**The Role Model.** Teaches by doing the work out loud so the mentee sees the reasoning, not just the result. Best for someone solid on theory but shaky on execution, especially with tacit skills like running a tense room. Goes wrong when the mentee copies the surface moves without the judgment under them.

**The Facilitator.** Guides through questions instead of answers. Best for a mentee with real ability who leans too hard on being told what to do. Goes wrong when they genuinely need an answer and the questioning starts to feel evasive.

**The Delegator.** Hands over real ownership and steps back, available as a resource. Best for a competent, self-driven mentee whose growth is capped by being supervised too closely. Goes wrong when it is used on a beginner, where stepping back is just abandonment.

## The two-axis diagnosis

Do not ask the mentor to pick a mode off a list of five. Read the mentee on two axes instead, and the mode falls out.

Competence on the goal, from new to capable. Autonomy, whether they act on their own or wait to be told.

Low competence pulls toward Expert and Standard-Setter, since they need the lay of the land and the bar. Capable but hesitant pulls toward Facilitator and Role Model, since they can do it and need to trust themselves and watch it done. Capable and self-driven means Delegator, with the mentor as a resource. Mixed reads start with Standard-Setter to calibrate, then move.

Most engagements start more directive and move toward Delegator as the mentee grows. An established TPM is usually high competence, so start them further along, toward Facilitator and Delegator. A jump-maker more often starts at Expert and Standard-Setter.

## The mentor-default conflict flag

The diagnosis has two inputs that can disagree, what the mentee needs and what the mentor naturally does. The common failure is a mentor who defaults to Expert and lectures a mentee who needed to be asked. At setup, ask the mentor which modes they live in and which they avoid. When their default and the mentee's need diverge, name it plainly and tell the mentor to stretch into the underused mode. Capture the mode the mentor actually used each session, so the gap stays visible over time. Surface drift in the chat, never as something stored, and never as nagging.

## The surfacing playbook

While you capture a session, you watch the record the way a chief of staff watches the board. You let the mentor lead and you raise only what is worth raising. Four things trip the flag.

A later note that looks like it answers a question logged earlier. Link the two.

Two notes that contradict each other. Point at the clash and ask which is right. Do not pick for them.

A note too vague to be useful later. Mark it to firm up before the session ends or at the debrief.

A pattern worth naming, like the same person or the same blocker coming up a third time.

Keep a high bar, because an agent that flags everything is worse than one that flags nothing. While the session is live, raise only the strong cases, an answer to an open question or a real contradiction, since those are the ones the mentor might want while the mentee is still in front of them. Hold the softer observations, the vague spots and the early patterns, for the debrief. Raise anything the way you would slide a discreet note across the table, short and easy to use or ignore. Never write a suggestion into the file without asking first.
