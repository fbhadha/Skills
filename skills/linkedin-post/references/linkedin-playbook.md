## How LinkedIn reads right now

Researched June 2026. The algorithm and the norms drift, so refresh this section every few months by re-running a search for current LinkedIn writing and formatting norms and rewriting what changed. Note the date when you do.

- The first two lines are the whole game. A post truncates at roughly 210 characters behind a see-more fold, so those two lines decide whether anyone reads the rest. They have to spark curiosity read cold. If you can guess the rest of the post from the opening line, the hook is weak.
- The 2025-2026 algorithm was rebuilt to cut viral but low-value content and lift genuine expertise from people in your network. Engagement bait now hurts reach: hashtag stuffing, "agree?" posts, and pods drag you down. Write from real knowledge, not for the like.
- Hooks that land in 2026: a contrarian line that challenges a common belief, a specific number, an unexpected comparison, or a real personal beat with some vulnerability. A natural open that starts on a person or a flat claim and turns structural sits in this lane.
- Aggressive white space reads better than dense blocks. One thought per line, generous line breaks. If the user's drafts run as prose paragraphs, then when the piece is going to the feed, break it into shorter line-separated beats while keeping the voice. Keep the prose form for the longer article length.
- There is a real backlash against AI-sounding posts. Certain punctuation and formatting now read as generated and get scrolled past, em dashes chief among them. The AI-tell pass below is not optional on LinkedIn, it is survival.
- No salesy pitch energy. Professionals smell a pitch and leave. Be the person with something worth reading, not the person selling.
- The 2026 algorithm reads your whole post with a semantic model, so the words in the body decide who sees it as much as any tag does. Say the thing plainly in the post and you have already done most of the work of telling the algorithm who it is for.

## Finding the real angle

The rough take the user hands you is the starting point, not the post. The strongest argument is usually sitting under the first framing rather than in it. Before you draft, say back the one thing you think the post is really about, and if their notes wander across several points or the obvious framing is weaker than something underneath it, ask which is the true angle. Keep asking, one question at a time, until they confirm it, then draft that one. A post built on the surface take when a sharper one was right there is the most common way one of these comes out flat.

## The AI-tell pass

Run this as a separate pass over the draft before the voice check. Cut or rewrite every hit. These are the patterns that read as machine-written, and on LinkedIn they get you scrolled past.

- Em and en dashes. Zero. Replace with commas, periods, or a rebuild. This is the single biggest tell.
- Three-beat list rhythm, the rule of three. "innovative, scalable, and transformative." Write two items or four, or fold them into a sentence.
- "Not X but Y" as a formula. Rewrite as a plain contrast across two sentences.
- Significance inflation: "stands as a testament," "marks a pivotal moment," "in today's evolving landscape," "underscores the importance of," "plays a vital role."
- Trailing -ing phrases that fake depth: "highlighting the need," "ensuring success," "reflecting a broader shift," "fostering alignment."
- Sycophantic openers: "Great question," "I hope this helps," "Happy to share."
- Inspirational closes: "the future is bright," "exciting times ahead," "onward and upward," any call-to-action uplift.
- Signposting: "In conclusion," "It's worth noting that," "Let's dive in," "First, let's explore."
- Promotional gloss: "seamless," "powerful," "robust," "game-changing," "unlock," "supercharge."
- Copula avoidance, dressing up plain "is" and "has" into "serves as" and "functions as." Use the plain verb.
- Any dead words the user flags on sight (record these in the voice section above). When a phrase feels like filler, cut it.
- Even, mid-length cadence with no short lines. Break it. Real writing alternates short and long.

After cutting, ask once: what here still reads as AI. Fix that, then move to the voice check.

## The voice check

Run after the AI-tell pass, before presenting. A no on any line means the draft is not done.

- The first two lines work as a hook read cold, under about 210 characters.
- Opens with a person, a specific, or a flat claim, not an abstraction or a wish.
- At least one ellipsis and at least one parenthetical present.
- At least one signature beat present: CAPS, a scare quote, or a characteristic phrase from the user's profile.
- Zero em or en dashes. Zero questions. Zero exclamations.
- No three-beat list rhythm.
- Sentence length actually varies, short next to long.
- Reading level, spelling variant, and register match the user's profile.
- One argument held across the whole post.
- The user's stance is intact, not softened or reversed.
- Specifics stayed specific, no real number rounded into a vague phrase.
- The close is a position or a concrete image, not a slogan or an uplift line.

## Hashtags

Researched June 2026, and the norms drift, so re-run a search for current LinkedIn hashtag practice every few months and rewrite what changed. Note the date when you do.

Do not bake a tag list into this skill. Tags get researched per post, because the right ones depend on the topic and the norms keep moving.

Where hashtags stand now. They are a minor lever, worth maybe a 2 to 5 percent reach bump, and they work as a label that tells the algorithm what the post is about rather than a feed anyone browses. LinkedIn removed hashtag following in 2024, so the follower counts you still see quoted are stale. The algorithm reads the whole post with a semantic model, which means the words in the body carry as much of the classification as the tags do.

How to pick them. Three to five is LinkedIn's own line, but the sharper 2026 read is to default to zero to three and only reach for four to five on topic-heavy or community posts. Past ten you risk a real visibility penalty. Specific beats broad by a wide margin, since broad tags like Leadership, Success, Motivation, or a bare AI confuse the classifier and pull the wrong crowd, while niche tags pull the right people. The first three tags get baked into the post URL, which feeds Google, so spend those three well, and put the tags at the end.

The method for a post. Search the current hashtag norms, then search tags for this post's actual topic. Recommend about three niche, on-topic tags and skip the broad ones. Then check whether the body even names the terms the classifier needs. When it does not, the tags are carrying that weight alone, which is fine when the post was kept plain on purpose. Do not stuff brand names into the body just to feed the classifier.
