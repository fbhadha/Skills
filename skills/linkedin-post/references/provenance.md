# Provenance: linkedin-post

Distilled from conversations that produced LinkedIn posts, then generalized and sanitized into a reusable skill on 2026-06-19. The original was calibrated to one writer; this version carries the method and the dimension structure, with the voice and examples held as templates the user fills during setup. No personal corpus or identifying data is included.

## Dimensions and the calls made

These record how the choice points have typically been resolved, so the skill can show a default and a reason. They are the prior writer's calls, not rules — surface them and let the current user choose.

### length
Options weighed: tight feed post around 200 words, fuller article around 450.
Typical call: it tends to grow as more ideas go in, so a piece can land at article length when the argument earns the room.
For next time: current LinkedIn favours the shorter hook-first post, so the skill defaults to the feed post unless the user says otherwise. The article length is there when the argument needs the room.

### close
Options weighed: a bookend back to the opening, a broader provocative line, a personal-choices ending.
Typical call: varies by piece. A personal-choices ending makes honesty land; a bookend pays off the opening; a broader provocative line takes the wider swing. Ask which the user wants this run.

### edge
Options weighed: measured for a watching employer, or sharper.
Typical call: measured. When the user has a job to protect, the sharper political or critical framing gets softened into observable points rather than a swipe. Confirm the user's appetite for edge before drafting.

## Quality gates and the reasoning behind them

### the hook
The standard: the first two lines spark curiosity read cold and survive the see-more fold.
Why it mattered: current LinkedIn truncates at about 210 characters, so the opening two lines are the whole chance to earn the read.

### no AI tells
The standard: nothing that reads as machine-written, em dashes and three-beat rhythms and filler words gone.
Why it mattered: LinkedIn now scrolls past posts that read as generated, so the AI-tell pass is survival, not polish.

### voice markers
The standard: at least one ellipsis, one parenthetical, and one signature beat (CAPS, a scare quote, or a characteristic phrase from the user's profile).
Why it mattered: these are what make a draft read like the user and not a style guide following itself.

### find the real angle
The standard: confirm the one argument the post is actually about before drafting, and when the rough take and the strongest point differ, ask which is the true angle.
Why it mattered: the surface framing is often weaker than a point sitting under it, and that only surfaces by questioning the take instead of drafting it straight.

## Branches tried and dropped

- Drafting the surface take without interrogating it: dropped, because the sharper angle is usually underneath the first framing.
- Naming a product in the post: dropped, since naming reads like a pitch on LinkedIn.
- A pure politics-and-capitalism critique when an employer is watching: softened to keep the user on safe footing. The user sets the edge.
- Relying on the general my-voice skill at run time: dropped for LinkedIn output, since this skill is standalone and overrides my-voice when it fires.
- Baking a fixed hashtag list into the skill: dropped, tags get researched per post.
- Stuffing brand names into the body to feed the classifier: dropped, the post stays plain and pitch-free.

## Notes for future updates

The LinkedIn norms and the hashtag snapshot in the playbook are dated June 2026 and the algorithm changes, so refresh them every few months. The real angle for any given post is discovered by interview, not assumed, so do not hard-code one. The voice section and examples in the playbook are templates: fill them from the user's own LinkedIn corpus using the my-voice onboarding method.
