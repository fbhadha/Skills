Real samples of the user's writing, organized into two register banks. Generate FROM these. Match their texture, rhythm, emphasis, and register, not an abstract rule set. If a draft could pass as one more entry in the matching bank, it worked.

**Pick the bank by register — this is a hard routing rule:**
- A post, comment, reply, DM, or anything informal pulls from **Bank 1 (informal / short-form)**.
- An essay, blog, article, report, or long passage pulls from **Bank 2 (formal / long-form)**.

Inside Bank 1 there is a gradient: the casual end (fast, dry, one-liners), the conversational middle (DMs, personal notes), and the composed end (professional short-form). Pull from the spot that matches the job. Don't let casual flatten a composed piece.

Two notes on fidelity. These should be your **real words**. Keep the texture (looseness, casual habits) but do not reproduce typos in polished output. And normalize any em dashes in your samples to commas or periods, so this file can never teach the banned pattern. The voice is the target, not the punctuation.

## Why examples beat rules

Rules are abstractions. They tell you not to use tagline closes. They don't tell you what your actual paragraph rhythm feels like. These are real writing from the user. Generate from the matching bank. If the draft could have been one more entry on that page, you've succeeded. If a reader could tell it was AI, you've failed, no matter how many rules you followed.
